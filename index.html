Joppa Neighborhood — Air Quality Model

Dallas, TX · Asphalt plants (2) emission simulation · Every slider updates all charts in real time

This tool models the Joppa Neighborhood in Dallas, Texas which has been the center of a long-running debate on air quality equity, Joppa was the first predominantly Black neighbourhood in Dallas and it has been classified as a ‘Sacrifice Zone’ for many years and the construction of the two asphalt plants is at the center of it. This tool models the ambient air quality (PM2.5, PM10, VOC) at 200m from two asphalt plants using EPA AP-42 emission factors and Gaussian plume dispersion. You can adjust throughput, baghouse efficiency, wind speed, and dust factors to explore how operational changes affect nearby residents. The Gaussian plume model is well-suited to this urban industrial scenario because it accounts for stack height, atmospheric stability, and how concentrations decline with distance. The tool produces real-time AQI values (combining PM2.5 and PM10), concentration maps, downwind dispersion profiles, and side-by-side comparisons across baseline, baghouse upgrade, and plant removal scenarios.

Reflection

With all of this data provided, I personally believe the best option is to rezone the area to disallow the industrial activity from occurring. While it is the less “engineer-y” solution it holds the most water in my opinion. Joppa is a community that has been overlooked for at this point over a century, some places here don’t even have running water. If one of these plants popped up in an affluent neighbourhood north of the interstate there would be outrage at the health concerns and safety of it. Thinking beyond just an engineering solution, this is a solution that would speak volumes, it would show that the city isn’t ignoring this community and that they are willing to fight for what's right for Joppa. I think the comparison holds this up, I did leave it open to adjustment but the numbers I have it set to are the ones I chose. One thing about the comparison that could be approved upon is having public input. The community’s voice is the one that should be heard and I would be most comfortable making a design choice with input from the community itself. Another place I think it breaks down is in the cost section, this is another place where I had to really guide the AI into making sure it was using correct data. I sourced many things from the City of Dallas, and beyond, to ensure that the data I was using for this tool would be accurate. One of the biggest factors in this comparison is the cost, I have it scaled the highest and it would undoubtedly be the most important to the stakeholders so I would want to be more confident in that assessment in order to better make a design choice. This tool felt like I finally was able to us AI to it’s best potential for me to build things. I used claude to give me an initial backbone and then tinkered with it until my tokens ran out finally landing on this version. The section where I had the most input was on the dispersion diagram, I had to rewrite and override the AI constantly in order to make it look the way I wanted it to. I felt like I was using claude code as an employee for this tool rather than it being the boss, I was making decisions and it was doing it, and then I would keep trying to make it to my liking. The ability to visualize the data was key for me and I think it did a great job displaying that, it just took a while to get it just right.
